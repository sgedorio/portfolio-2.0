WREK Radio Website Redesign (Desktop & Mobile)
By Sean Gedorio & Shireen Hashemi

Overview

Our website redesign for Georgia Tech’s college radio station, WREK Atlanta, addresses the site’s weaknesses in how content is organized and how inflexible its current design is for smaller devices.

The responsive, grid-based design that we propose tidies up the existing information architecture while bringing life to WREK’s eccentric personality for both desktop and mobile environments.

What is the Problem?

WREK’s website first went live in 1993, long before the Internet meant anything to anyone outside of academia and technical trades. Since then, only 3 redesigns have been implemented, none of which anticipated the ubiquity of mobile phones or the growing importance of UX.

Run by passionate engineering students for the past 50 years, WREK has always been a bastion of technological achievement. Currently it runs at 100,000 watts, making it one of the most powerful college radio stations across the country. Despite its technical dominance, communication in terms of aesthetics, branding, and good user experience has been weak, especially in recent times.

As Publicity Director, I had the privilege of reshaping the station’s creative direction and strengthening its digital presence.

Fig. 1. Progression of WREK's website over the years from 1993 (top left) to now (bottom right)

The consensus from the station’s managers was that the current website needs to be relooked at and improved. Right now, the information is organized quite chaotically, with redundant navigation features and confusing page titles. The current layout also isn't responsive to accommodate for our growing mobile traffic.

I co-led this project with my friend, Shireen, in hopes of learning users’ current problems with the site to make significant improvements on their experiences. Our 3 main goals were to:

  1. Design desktop and mobile interfaces that are intuitive and aesthetically pleasing.
  2. Reorganize current site architecture to promote seamless flow of website exploration.
  3. Have fun learning Figma! (as previous Sketch users who wanted to know what the hype was all about)

Affinity Diagram

We created an affinity diagram from the user survey results to extract key ideas that needed to be kept in mind when designing the website. 3-5 ideas were generated from each submitted survey. Through a quick, intuitive, holistic review, these ideas were sorted into related groups.

Fig. 3. Pictures from our Affinity Diagramming Session

We had a total of around 20 niches of ideas which we compiled into 3 main takeaways:

  1. Parts of the site architecture are unintuitive and confusing
  2. Key website features should be made more visibly clear and obvious
  3. The website is functional, but lacks visual and written personality (branding problem)

Branding Activity

In general, branding for student organizations (especially the ones that don’t focus on design) is never truly consistent.  Creative direction fluctuates depending on the skills at hand of current members—none of whom are expected to have a background in design or marketing when joining a college radio station.

WREK Design Collective was my attempt at solidifying a foundation for how future generations should approach the station brand and image. I knew that the website redesign needed to strongly reflect our station values in both aesthetic and function.

Fig. 5. Group branding activity conducted at a Wrek Design Collective meeting

For one of our meetings, I conducted a group branding activity to get a group consensus of how exactly the station’s identity be perceived.

Using post-it notes and markers, I had everyone write down as many adjectives as they could think of to describe WREK (one word per note). This was a rapid 5 minute activity to get the most spontaneous and intuitive answers. Everyone stuck their words up on the wall and were given time to read each other’s answers. We discussed as a group and voted on the top 3 words that best reflected our station’s core values:

  •  Quality 
  • Inclusive
  •  Eclectic

In order to build a strong brand, any piece of artwork or communication from our station should be able to pass through and represent the words quality, inclusive, and eclectic. This pertains to our website as well—along with implementing data from our research, we needed to ensure that the key components of the site reflected these 3 core values as well.

Reorganizing Site Architecture

To be more inclusive, we needed to make sure that navigation was as intuitive as possible for prospective members and first-time listeners.

Insightful responses from the survey pointed out that the dropdown menu labels could be more clear. Upon further inspection, the menu had a lot of confusing and contradictory features.

Fig. 6. The existence of a "More" tab indicates that the current navigation tabs need to be reconsidered

For example, a new listener may use the website to check out recently played songs. They must first choose between the “Music” or “Shows” tab, which is already confusing since our shows encompass every part of the music that we play. Even after the user correctly selects “Music”, they need to select “Playlist”. This isn’t clear because the station constantly refers to playlists in the context of specialty show playlists, which have their own separate space in the website.

Rapid Prototyping & Wireframing

Fig. 8. Rapid paper prototyping sessions allowed us to flesh out a wide range of ideas in a short amount of time

In the early stages of conceptualizing the potential layout for the website, we wanted to quickly visualize and test various ideas in a low cost and commitment manner. Paper prototyping allowed for rapid iteration—only allowing 5 minutes to come up with a design.

Grid for Aesthetic Longevity

Throughout our rapid paper prototyping process, we kept in mind functionality, clarity, and visibility with each iteration. Some layouts emphasized the web player, others the programming schedule—our main goal was to make the design universal to our 4 personas. We wanted to make a layout that encompassed all of the key features without resulting in an information overload.

Our affinity diagrams and personas revealed that the top 5 most common reasons to use the site were to:

  1. Check for recent specialty show playlists
  2. View the schedule/programming for the day
  3. Stream online
  4. Find out what is currently playing on air
  5. Get involved

As we finished our paper prototypes and started our early wireframing process, we made sure to include these key features on the homepage to minimize information access cost.

Fig. 9. Early iteration wireframe of the Home page focusing on the top user needs (top), iterations of sticky left navigation sidebar (bottom)

We agreed that the most ideal layout to accommodate all of WREK’s content would be a grid format. Several considerations were kept in mind when making this decision, such as how difficult development would be and whether or not this layout would make us too different from other sites.

With the rise of CSS Grid, grid layouts have been increasingly prevalent in recent years, allowing for a brutalist approach that achieves aesthetics without sacrificing pragmatic functionality. Brutalist layouts are often designed to “break away from the stale, cookie-cutter, premade-template sites that dominate the web today.”

Fig. 10. Grid-based websites that we were inspired by

Even though a grid layout was a creatively challenging approach, we questioned whether its unconventionality would deter users. Jakob’s Law states that users spend most of their time on other sites. Websites need to tone down their distinct appearances since standardized design allows for more ease of experience. Not even considering the grid, our left sticky desktop sidebar is unusual to most users who are more familiar with a sticky navigation menu at the top of the page.

After careful assessment of our station’s history, philosophy, and branding, we decided to push forward anyways.

For several years, the station’s unofficial motto has been “Music you don’t hear on the radio” — WREK is historically notorious for unabashedly rejecting commercial music and uplifting less popular forms of music into public consciousness. We decided the website should be consistent with our music philosophy by being unconventional in design. Still keeping the users in mind, our research consistently showed that many users were put off by the current website’s lack of visual appeal. Many wanted WREK’s eccentric personality to be reflected in the website.

A grid layout would also strongly sustain our station’s eclectic nature; having explicit borders around different and varied content can easily improve users’ navigation throughout the website.

Fig. 11. Examples of completed desktop and mobile wireframes

Overall, a grid allowed us to be simple yet unconventional, functional yet stimulating, and consistent yet distinct.

Reflection

We learned a lot of valuable lessons throughout the course of this project, the top 5 being:

1. Constantly ask for feedback. Don’t be afraid to be resourceful.


After conducting surveys, affinity diagrams, and personas, Shireen and I thought it would be best to get feedback from an experienced professional to legitimize our progress. I thought, why not the person who taught me these skills in the first place? I contacted my professor from my human-centered design class and to my surprise, she agreed to meet up with us over coffee.

Not only did she provide great constructive feedback of our progress thus far, she also proposed the next steps we should take moving forward, such as our branding activity.

Taking advantage of this industry’s mentorship culture and asking for varied critique throughout several phases of the product is key to delivering a desirable outcome.

2. Remote usability tests save a lot of time and energy, but they also come with significant drawbacks.


Another activity our professor suggested to conduct was an open card sorting activity to figure out how to reorganize the current information architecture. She specifically stressed on selecting external participants not involved with the station—asking WREK members who already know how the site is laid out may skew the results.

For convenience, we decided to conduct our card sorting online through OptimalSort. We tested a total of 10 student participants, all of whom were uninvolved with the station.

Fig. 20. OptimalSort card sorting activity to gain insight on navigation tab organization

Holistically, the responses were scattered and showed no useful patterns. This could be due to the fact that most pages on the site rely on a background understanding of how the station operates. Participants couldn’t ask us for help or clarification.

It was through this activity that we learned the perils of remote usability tests, specifically unmoderated ones. We learned that it’s in the best interest of any project to conduct tests with as much human interaction as possible (especially if the time constraints allow for it). This is becoming a troublesome problem for designers in the COVID era, but even remote tests can be improved through attentive moderation.

3. Be thorough with wireframing (or you’ll regret it), but understand that iteration is inevitable.


While designing the mobile prototype, we opted for a state-switch button for the play/stop controls in order to save space for the sticky header. It was only when prototyping the mobile version that we realized this is actually the universal convention for play/stop controls. Our desktop version included both the play and stop buttons beside each other, which may be confusing for users who need to learn that the state is determined by the button’s color (it is made more confusing considering the design is flat).

Fig. 21. Play and stop states of Desktop online web player determined by color of button

This could have been prevented had we been more thorough with the wireframing process. We paid more attention to the simplicity and compactness of the sidebar as a whole rather than the functionality of the play/stop controls.

We plan on figuring out how to transition to a 2-state button or make clearer visual indicators between stop and play for the next design iteration. It might even be useful to see if our current design is already feasible through usability tests.

4. Always keep implementation in mind. Research insights need to be as transparent as possible to other teams.


One of the three core values we agreed WREK embodied from our branding activity was quality.

While the data showed that most users were put off by the current website’s lack of organization and personality, a significant portion was also frustrated with many broken site functionalities (e.g. buggy links/buttons/databases).

A quality redesign consists of not only presenting new feasible models, but also ensuring that the infrastructures of the existing one function properly. These broken site functionalities need to be addressed with the engineers before moving forward with the redesign process. To polish a product on the surface level while neglecting the rot in its core is like building a house without a foundation—in due time, these structures will ultimately crumble.

The broader lesson I’ve learned is that design teams should never be insular. They should be as transparent as possible, constantly collaborating with other teams to relay important research insights. To deliver quality, the design handoff should deliver not just fancy, pretentious results, but gritty data as well. We need to focus more on the why than the what.

5. Figma is superior.
